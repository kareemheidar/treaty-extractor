Your build guide

Treaty Extractor — build it in Retool, step by step

A friendly, click-by-click guide to building your hackathon app. No coding or AI background needed — every step tells you exactly what to click in Retool and what it does. Work through it in order and you'll have a working app by the end.

For: the build team (3–5 people) No experience required You'll use AI, made simple

What are we building?

Right now, after a reinsurance treaty (a contract) is signed, someone reads the whole document by hand and re-types the important details into other systems. It's slow and easy to get wrong. Your app will do the reading for them: upload the treaty → the app pulls out the key details and the rate tables → a person checks them on screen → clicks Approve → the clean data is saved and can be exported.

The goal

One working slice you can show off — upload a treaty, watch it fill in, approve it. Not a finished product.

How to use this

Follow the parts in order. Each step has a Goal, the exact clicks, and a Checkpoint to confirm it worked.

Golden rule

Use practice (mock) data only — never a real client's treaty. And keep everything in English letters/numbers.

Two promises to make this easier

You don't need to memorise anything — copy the snippets we give you and move on. And you won't get stuck alone: your coach stays with you the whole time and there's support on standby. If a step doesn't behave, wave them over.

Learn the words once

1 · Retool basics (5-minute primer)

Retool is a tool for building simple apps by dragging pieces onto a screen — no coding from scratch. Here are the only words you need, in plain English. You'll meet each one again when you use it.

App

The thing you're building — a screen with buttons, tables, and boxes. You build it in the editor.

Component

A ready-made piece you drag onto the screen: a button, a table, a text box, a file upload. You pick them from the component list on the right.

Query

An action the app runs — "read the file", "ask the AI", "save to the database". They live in the panel at the bottom of the editor.

Resource

A connection the app uses — the AI service and the database are each a resource. Your admin has set these up already.

Retool Database

A built-in place to store your data in tables (like a spreadsheet with rules). This is where approved treaties get saved.

AI action

A special query that asks an AI model to do something — read a document, or pull out fields. You just fill in a box; no AI knowledge needed.

Transformer

A tiny helper that tidies up data from a query so it shows nicely in a table. You'll copy ours in.

Event handler

A rule that says "when this happens, do that." Example: "when the AI finishes, save the result." You add these in a few clicks.

The editor at a glance

When you open an app you'll see: the canvas (your screen) in the middle, the component list on the right, the query panel at the bottom, and the inspector (settings) on the right when you click something. That's it — you'll use these four areas the whole time.

The basic moves — how to actually do things

Every step in this guide is built from these six little actions. When a step says "add a component" or "create a query", the exact clicks are right here. Come back any time.

Move 1 — Add a component (drag & drop)
  1. Look at the right side of the editor — that's the component library, full of little tiles (Button, Table, Text…).
  2. Don't see it? Click the squares icon (or "+ Add") at the top-right to open it.
  3. Find the piece you want — scroll, or type its name in the search box at the top of that panel.
  4. Point your mouse at the tile, press and hold the left mouse button, drag it into the middle area (the canvas), and let go where you want it.
  5. It appears on your screen. That's it.
Move 2 — Select something & find its name
  1. Click a component once to select it. A settings panel opens on the right — the Inspector.
  2. At the very top of the Inspector is the component's name. Retool names things automatically, like fileInput1, table1, button1.
  3. To rename it, click the name and type. (Keeping the auto-name is fine too — just use whatever name it shows in your snippets.)
Move 3 — Type into a box, and what {{ }} means
  1. Settings in the Inspector are boxes you type into (like "Value", "Text", "Label").
  2. Plain words go in as-is. But double curly braces {{ }} mean "put a live value here", not literal text.
  3. Example: {{ fileInput1.value[0].name }} means "the name of the uploaded file." Copy our snippets exactly, including the {{ }}.
Move 4 — Create a query (an action)
  1. Look at the bottom of the editor — the query panel. If it's shut, click it to open it.
  2. Click + New (or "+ Add") → choose Resource query.
  3. At the top of the new query is a Resource dropdown. Click it and pick the connection you need (the AI resource, the Retool S3 bucket, or the Retool Database).
  4. Fill in the boxes it shows you, then rename the query (top-left) so you remember what it does.
Move 5 — Run a query & check it worked
  1. With a query open, click the Run button (a play ▶ icon, top-right of the query).
  2. The answer appears in the Output area at the bottom of the query. Green means it worked; red means something's off — wave your coach over.
Move 6 — Add an event handler ("when this, do that")
  1. Select the component or query that should react (e.g. a button, or a query that just finished).
  2. In the Inspector, scroll to Event handlers. Click + Add.
  3. Pick the Event (like "Click", "Success", "Row clicked") and the Action (like "Trigger query", "Open component"), then choose the target.

Plain meaning: "when this thing happens, do that thing automatically."

Goal for this part: you can upload a treaty PDF, the app stores it, and you can see its text on screen. Five small steps — every click is spelled out. When a step mentions "add a component" or "create a query", the exact clicks are in Section 1 → The basic moves.

  1. Go to your Retool home page. Click the Create new button (top-right corner) and choose App.
  2. A small box asks for a name. Type Treaty Extractor and confirm.
  3. You're now in the editor. Middle = the canvas (your screen). Right = the component library. Bottom = the query panel.
Checkpoint: you're looking at an empty app editor with your app's name at the top.
Step A2 — Add the file-upload box (drag & drop)App & Screens
Goal: a button people click to choose a treaty file. (Uses Move 1.)
  1. On the right side (the component library), click the search box at the top and type file.
  2. Find the tile called File Input (or "File Button"). Press and hold it with your mouse, drag it into the middle (the canvas), and let go.
  3. A file box now sits on your screen. Click it once to select it.
  4. Look at the top of the Inspector (right side) — it shows the box's name. It's almost certainly fileInput1. We'll use that exact name in the snippets below, so if yours is different, either rename it to fileInput1 or swap the name in the snippets.
Checkpoint: a file-upload box is on your canvas and named fileInput1.
Step A3 — Upload a treaty & read its name and contentsApp & Screens
Goal: put a practice file in and prove the app can read it. (Uses Moves 1 & 3.)
  1. On the canvas, click your file box and pick the practice treaty PDF from your computer. (You can do this right here in the editor.)
  2. Now prove the app sees it: drag a Text component onto the canvas (search text in the component library).
  3. Click the Text component. In the Inspector, find the Value box, clear it, and paste exactly:
    {{ fileInput1.value[0].name }}
  4. The file's name should now show on screen. That means the upload worked.

What these mean (important — you'll reuse them):

  • {{ fileInput1.value[0].name }} = the name of the uploaded file (e.g. "Sample_Treaty.pdf").
  • {{ fileInput1.value[0].base64Data }} = the file's contents, turned into safe text called base64. Other steps use this to actually read the treaty. (You don't need to understand base64 — just know this is "the file itself.")
Checkpoint: the Text component shows your file's name.
Step A4 — Create the file query (Retool S3 bucket)App & Screens
Goal: a query that takes the uploaded file and stores it so the app can work with it. (Uses Moves 4 & 5.)
  1. At the bottom (query panel), click + New → Resource query.
  2. At the top of the new query, open the Resource dropdown and choose Retool S3 bucket.
  3. Find the Bucket name field and leave it empty — don't type anything. (Empty means "use the default storage.")
  4. In the box for the file's data, paste the contents: {{ fileInput1.value[0].base64Data }}
  5. In the box for the file name, paste: {{ fileInput1.value[0].name }}
  6. Rename the query (top-left) to uploadFile, then click Run.

What this does: takes the file you uploaded and saves it into Retool's storage. The two boxes you filled are just "the file's contents" and "the file's name", read straight from the upload box in Step A3.

Heads-up: box labels can look slightly different depending on the setup. If a field name doesn't match exactly (e.g. "Data" vs "File content"), ask your coach to point at the right one — the two things you're putting in are always the base64 contents and the file name.

Checkpoint: click Run — the query finishes green (no red error).
Step A5 — Turn the PDF into textAI & Extraction
Goal: get the words out of the PDF so the app can use them. (Uses Moves 4 & 5.)
  1. Query panel → + New → Resource query.
  2. Resource dropdown → your AI resource (your admin named it — it runs on AWS Bedrock for this event).
  3. Set Action to Convert document to text.
  4. In File source, paste: {{ fileInput1.value[0] }}
  5. Rename the query to convertDoc, then click Run.
  6. Drag a Text Area component onto the canvas and set its Value to {{ convertDoc.data }} so you can see the text.

What this does: reads the PDF and gives back its text as words the app can use next.

Checkpoint: after Run, the treaty's text appears in the Text Area.

If "Convert document to text" isn't in the Action list, that feature may not be switched on for the event. No problem — use Plan B below instead. Everything after this step works exactly the same.

Step A5 (Plan B) — Read the PDF with a small script in a WorkflowAI & Extraction
Goal: the same result as A5 — the treaty's text — but using a tiny script instead of the built-in AI action. Only do this if A5 wasn't available.

What's a Workflow? It's a separate mini-program in Retool that runs a few steps in order, away from your app screen. We'll make one whose only job is: take the PDF, read its text, hand it back. Your app calls it like any other action.

  1. In Retool, go to Create new → Workflow. Name it extractText.
  2. It starts with a Start trigger block. This is where your app will send the PDF.
  3. Add a Code block and set its language to Python. Paste the script below into it.
  4. Open the Workflow's settings → Python libraries and add pypdf (this is the tool that reads PDFs). If it's already there, skip this.
  5. Back in your app: query panel → + New → Resource query → choose your extractText Workflow. In its input, set file to {{ fileInput1.value[0].base64Data }} (the file's contents). Name this query extractText and Run.
  6. Show the result: a Text Area with Value {{ extractText.data.text }}.

Paste this into the Python code block

# Reads the words out of the uploaded PDF
import base64, io
from pypdf import PdfReader

# 1. The app sends the PDF as base64 (a text-safe way to send a file).
raw = startTrigger.data["file"]

# 2. Turn that text back into the real PDF file.
pdf_bytes = base64.b64decode(raw)

# 3. Open the PDF and read the text from every page.
reader = PdfReader(io.BytesIO(pdf_bytes))
pages = [(p.extract_text() or "") for p in reader.pages]

# 4. Send all the text back to the app.
return {"text": "\n\n".join(pages)}

What each part does, in plain words:

  • import… — brings in pypdf, the free tool that knows how to read PDFs.
  • Step 1 — grabs the file your app sent in.
  • Step 2 — files travel as scrambled text (called base64); this un-scrambles it back into a real PDF.
  • Step 3 — opens the PDF and pulls the words off each page.
  • Step 4 — joins the pages together and hands the text back to your app.

One thing to remember: later steps in this guide say to use {{ convertDoc.data[0] }}. If you used Plan B, use {{ extractText.data.text }} in those spots instead — it's the same treaty text, just from the Workflow.

Checkpoint: the Text Area shows the treaty's text, just like in A2.
Part B · owner: AI & Extraction

5 · Part B — Extract the details with AI

Goal for this part: the treaty's fields and its rate table show up as neat tables. This is the AI heart of the app — take it slowly, copy our snippets, and test after each step.

Step B1 — Ask the AI for the treaty fieldsAI & Extraction
Goal: turn the messy text into labelled fields (treaty name, dates, percentages…). (Uses Moves 4 & 5.)
  1. At the bottom (query panel), click + New → Resource query.
  2. At the top of the new query, open the Resource dropdown and choose your AI resource (the one your admin set up — it runs on AWS Bedrock).
  3. Find the Action dropdown and choose Generate text.
  4. Find the Model dropdown and pick the Claude model your admin enabled (for example claude-sonnet-4-6).
  5. Find the box labelled System message and paste the first block below into it (this tells the AI exactly what to give back).
  6. Find the box labelled Prompt (sometimes "User message") and paste the second block below.
  7. At the top-left of the query, click its name and change it to extractProducts.
  8. Click Run (the ▶ button, top-right of the query).

A treaty (addendum) covers a few products (the client's sample has 3). It has one benefit table that lists the benefits, and those same benefits apply to every product. We want one row per product, and inside each product the shared list of benefits. So the AI returns a list of products.

Paste into "System message"

You are a reinsurance treaty analyst. A treaty (addendum) covers one or more
PRODUCTS. It has ONE benefit table listing the BENEFITS, and those benefits
apply to EVERY product in the addendum.

Extract EVERY product. Return ONLY a raw JSON ARRAY (no markdown, no code
fences) where each element is one product with EXACTLY these keys:

treaty_number, fg, treaty_id, amendment, plan_code, issue_date,
term_date_of_nb, product_name, benefits, issue_age, waiting_period,
claim_approval_limit, sum_assured, nar_used_in_rga_calculation, commission,
ceding_type, ceding_retention, rga_share, rate_guarantee, retention_basis,
aar_in_waiting_period, claim_in_waiting_period, coverage, channel, er,
auto_binding_limits, reprem_collection_status, number_of_diseases,
max_number_of_claims, number_of_disease_groups, jumbo_limit, claim_notice, notes

"benefits" is an ARRAY OF OBJECTS taken from the treaty's benefit table.
Attach the SAME benefit list to every product. Each benefit object is:
  {
    "benefit_name": string,                    // "Reinsured Benefits" column
    "calculation_of_net_risk_amount": string,  // "Calculation of Policy Net Amount at Risk"
    "collection_waiver_of_reprem": string,     // "Collection and waiver of reinsurance premium"
    "table": string                            // the "Table" column, e.g. "Table 1"
  }
Do NOT invent "commission" or "rate_table_id" — those get added afterwards.

Rules:
- term_date_of_nb is also called Hi_Issue_Date.
- claim_approval_limit is also called Ceding_Handling_Limit.
- Keep the original language of the text. If a field is missing, use null. Never guess.

Paste into "Prompt"

Extract every product and the shared benefit table from the
treaty text below.

TREATY TEXT:
{{ convertDoc.data[0] }}

What this does: the AI reads the text and returns the products, each carrying the list of benefits (name, net-risk calculation, reprem collection/waiver, and which rate table applies).

Checkpoint: after Run, the Output area shows a list of products, each with a benefits list inside.
Step B2 — Tidy the AI's answer (a transformer)AI & Extraction
Goal: clean the AI's answer and add the two columns the treaty's benefit table doesn't have — commission and rate_table_id.

The treaty's benefit table is missing three columns: product name (we get that from the selected product later), commission (each benefit inherits it from its product), and rate_table_id (built from the amendment + the table number). This transformer fills the last two in automatically.

  1. At the bottom (query panel), click + New, then choose Transformer.
  2. At the top-left, rename it to extractClean.
  3. Select all the sample code, delete it, and paste the snippet below.
  4. It runs on its own. Look at the Output underneath — each benefit now has a commission and a rate_table_id.
// 1) clean the AI answer, then 2) add commission + rate_table_id per benefit
let raw = extractProducts.data;                 // your Generate text query
let products;
if (raw && typeof raw === 'object') products = raw;
else {
  const s = String(raw).trim()
      .replace(/^```[a-z]*\s*/i, '').replace(/\s*```$/, '').trim();
  try { products = JSON.parse(s); } catch (e) { return { error: e.message }; }
}
if (!Array.isArray(products)) return [];

return products.map(p => ({
  ...p,
  benefits: (p.benefits || []).map(b => {
    const n = (String(b.table || '').match(/\d+/) || [''])[0];   // "Table 1" -> "1"
    return {
      ...b,
      commission: b.commission ?? p.commission ?? null,          // inherit from product
      rate_table_id: b.rate_table_id
        ?? (p.amendment && n ? p.amendment + "_RT" + n + "_M" : null)  // e.g. Addendum_11_RT1_M
    };
  })
}));

Why this exists: it removes any extra symbols the AI adds and quietly fills the two missing columns so your benefits table is complete. Just paste it. (If you named your Generate-text query something other than extractProducts, change that one word on line 2.)

Checkpoint: the output shows the products, and every benefit now has a commission and a rate_table_id (like Addendum_11_RT1_M).
Step B3 — Show the products in a tableApp & Screens
Goal: the products appear on screen in a table (this is the product_info sheet). (Uses Moves 1, 2 & 3.)
  1. On the right (component library), search table and drag a Table onto the canvas.
  2. Click the table to select it. In the Inspector (right), find the Data box.
  3. Clear the Data box and paste {{ extractClean.value }} — this is the list of products, so you get one row per product (the sample has 3).
  4. At the top of the Inspector, rename the table to productsTable.
Checkpoint: each product shows up as a row. There's a benefits column that holds a list — you'll show that nicely in the drawer next.

Step B4 — Show a product's benefits (quick check)App & Screens
Goal: click a product → see its benefits, so you can confirm the AI worked. (The polished drawer version comes in Part D — this is just a quick check.) (Uses Moves 1, 2 & 3.)
  1. On the right, search table and drag a second Table onto the canvas, under the products table.
  2. Rename it benefitsTable. Set its Data to {{ productsTable.selectedRow.benefits }}.
  3. Now click a product row in productsTable — the benefits of that product fill the benefits table.

What you should see: the benefits table has columns for benefit_name, calculation_of_net_risk_amount, collection_waiver_of_reprem, table, commission, and rate_table_id — the last two filled in by the transformer in Step B2. (Product name isn't here on purpose; it comes from the selected product.)

Checkpoint: clicking a product shows its benefits, and every benefit has a commission and a rate_table_id.
Part C · owner: Data & Database

6 · Part C — Save treaties to the database

Goal for this part: every treaty you analyse gets saved automatically so it shows up in a list you can come back to.

Step C1 — Design & create the storage tableData & Database
Goal: a table (like a spreadsheet with rules) to hold every treaty.

We store one row per uploaded treaty, and keep the whole extraction (all products, each with its benefits) inside one json column. This is the fastest design and it matches the app perfectly — the products table and the drawer read from that column. Build exactly this:

id
auto number — the unique key (Retool adds this for you)
treaty_ref
text — a label for the list (we'll use the first product's amendment)
cedent
text — a label for the list (we'll use the first product's name)
source_file
text — the uploaded file name
status
text — either in_review or approved
extracted_json
json — the AI's answer: the list of products, each with its benefits inside (this holds both sheets)
reviewed_json
json — the human-checked version of the products
reviewed_by
text — who approved it
reviewed_at
timestamp — when it was approved
created_at
timestamp — when it was first analysed

Why one json column instead of many? The two output sheets — product_info and benefit_rate_set — are naturally nested (each product has benefits). Storing them together as extracted_json means the app can show products in a table and benefits in the drawer with zero extra plumbing. (If you ever want proper separate tables, the "next step" version is two tables: products, and benefits with a product_id link — but that's not needed for the hackathon.)

  1. Leave the app for a moment. In the far-left sidebar of Retool, click Retool Database (a database/cylinder icon). Can't spot it? Ask your coach to point at it.
  2. Click Create new → Table. In the name box, type treaties.
  3. For each row in the list above, add a column: type the column name (e.g. treaty_ref), then choose its type from the dropdown next to it — text, json, or timestamp.
  4. id and created_at are usually created for you automatically — if you see them already there, leave them.
  5. Save the table.

Why two "json" columns? We keep the AI's first answer (extracted_json) separate from the human-checked one (reviewed_json) so we never lose the original — and later you can see how good the AI was.

Checkpoint: an empty treaties table exists with all the columns.
Step C2 — Build the "save" actionData & Database
Goal: an action that writes one treaty into the table. (Uses Moves 4 & 5.)
  1. Go back to your app. At the bottom (query panel), click + New → Resource query.
  2. Open the Resource dropdown and choose Retool Database.
  3. Near the top of the query, find the GUI / SQL toggle and set it to GUI. (GUI mode lets you fill in boxes instead of writing code.)
  4. Set Action type to Insert a record, and set Table to treaties.
  5. In the Changeset area, click + Add once per field below. For each: pick the column name on the left, and paste its value on the right:
    treaty_ref = {{ extractClean.value[0].amendment }} (e.g. Addendum_11)
    cedent = {{ extractClean.value[0].product_name }}
    source_file = {{ fileInput1.value[0].name }}
    status = in_review (plain text, no braces)
    extracted_json = {{ extractClean.value }}
  6. Rename the query (top-left) to insertTreaty.

What this does: saves the whole uploaded treaty as one row, marked in_review. The full list of products — with every product's benefits inside — is stored in extracted_json; treaty_ref and cedent are just quick labels for the list (taken from the first product). (The client's field list has no "cedent", so we show the product name there instead.)

Checkpoint: click Run once — a new row appears in your treaties table.
Step C3 — Make it save automatically (event handler)Data & Database
Goal: the moment the AI finishes, the treaty is saved — no extra click. (Uses Move 6.)
  1. At the bottom, click your extractProducts query to open it.
  2. In its settings, scroll down to Event handlers and click + Add.
  3. Set Event to Success (meaning "after this finishes without error").
  4. Set Action to Control query, set the method to Trigger, and choose insertTreaty.

What this does: an event handler is a "when this, do that" rule. Here: when the AI finishes reading the treaty, save it automatically.

Checkpoint: upload a fresh treaty and run extraction — a new row appears in treaties by itself.

Step D2 — Add the three status filtersApp & Screens
Goal: buttons to switch between In Review, Approved, and All. (Uses Moves 1, 2 & 3.)
  1. On the right, search segmented and drag a Segmented Control onto the canvas, just above the list table.

  3. Find Default value and set it to In Review. Rename the control to statusFilter.
  4. Now filter the list: click treatiesTable, open its Add filters setting, and add a rule — show rows where status is {{ statusFilter.value === 'All' ? undefined : statusFilter.value.toLowerCase().replace(' ', '_') }}.

What that snippet means: the buttons say "In Review / Approved / All", but the database stores in_review / approved. The snippet turns the button text into the stored word (and shows everything for "All"). If it feels fiddly, ask your coach.

Step E2 — Check the data before approving (validation)Data & Database
Goal: stop obvious mistakes — blank required fields, wrong formats.
  1. At the bottom (query panel), click + New, then choose Transformer.
  2. Rename it (top-left) to validate.
  3. Select all the sample code in the box, delete it, and paste the snippet below. It runs on its own.
// returns ok:true only when every product has the basics
const products = extractClean.value || [];
const problems = [];
if (!Array.isArray(products) || products.length === 0) {
  problems.push("No products were found");
} else {
  products.forEach((p, i) => {
    if (!p.product_name) problems.push("Product " + (i+1) + ": missing product_name");
    if (!Array.isArray(p.benefits) || p.benefits.length === 0)
      problems.push("Product " + (i+1) + ": no benefits listed");
  });
}
return { ok: problems.length === 0, problems };

What this does: a simple checklist the app runs for you. If anything's wrong, validate.value.problems lists it.

Checkpoint: validate.value.ok is true for a good treaty.

Step E5 — Export to Excel: product_info + benefit_rate_set (a sheet each)Data & Database
Goal: one button that downloads a single Excel workbook matching the client's output — a product_info sheet and a benefit_rate_set sheet.

Retool builds multi-sheet Excel with a small free tool called SheetJS. Set it up once, then it's one button.

  1. Add the tool: go to your app's Settings → Libraries, click + Add, and paste this URL:
    https://cdnjs.cloudflare.com/ajax/libs/xlsx/0.18.5/xlsx.full.min.js
    This gives you something called XLSX to use.
  2. At the bottom (query panel), click + New → JavaScript query. Rename it exportExcel.
  3. Delete the sample code and paste the code below.
  4. Drag a Button onto the canvas, set its Text to Export to Excel, and add an Event handler: Event Click → Action Control query → Trigger → exportExcel.

Paste this into the JavaScript query

// ───────── EDIT THIS PART ─────────
// Where the products come from. Use the review drawer's products,
// or the live extraction (extractClean.value) if you're testing.
const products = drawerProducts.data || extractClean.value || [];
// ──────── you can stop editing here ────────

// Sheet 1 (product_info): the products, without the nested benefits column.
const product_info = products.map(({ benefits, ...p }) => p);

// Sheet 2 (benefit_rate_set): every benefit, tagged with its product name.
const benefit_rate_set = products.flatMap(p =>
  (p.benefits || []).map(b => ({ product_name: p.product_name, ...b }))
);

const SHEETS = { "product_info": product_info, "benefit_rate_set": benefit_rate_set };

const wb = XLSX.utils.book_new();                       // new empty workbook
Object.entries(SHEETS).forEach(([name, rows]) => {
  const ws = XLSX.utils.json_to_sheet(rows || []);      // data -> sheet
  XLSX.utils.book_append_sheet(wb, ws, name.slice(0, 31)); // names max 31 chars
});

const base64 = XLSX.write(wb, { bookType: "xlsx", type: "base64" });
utils.downloadFile({ data: { base64Binary: base64 } }, "treaty_export", "xlsx");

Why the flatten? Benefits live inside each product. The benefit_rate_set sheet needs one row per benefit with its product name, so we spread the products out into a flat list. To add more sheets later: add another line to SHEETS — "sheet name": someArray.

Checkpoint: click the button — an .xlsx downloads with a product_info tab and a benefit_rate_set tab (including commission & rate_table_id).

If the download doesn't start: some Retool setups want a slightly different last line — utils.downloadFile(base64, "treaty_export", "xlsx"). Try that, or ask your coach; everything above it stays the same.

Only if you have spare time

9 · UI polish (nice-to-have)

If the app works and you have time, these quick touches make it look finished — no custom code needed. Skip anything you're short on time for; a working plain app beats a pretty broken one.

Group things in Containers

Drag a Container around related components and give it a title (e.g. "Upload", "All treaties"). Instantly tidier.

Colour the status column

On the list table, set the status column type to Tag — Retool colours the values, so Approved/In Review stand out.

Show benefit names as tags

In the products table, the benefits column is a list — set its type to Tags so it shows neat chips instead of raw text.

Add a title bar

Put a Text component at the top: "Treaty Extractor". Big heading style. Gives the app an identity.

Friendly labels

Rename buttons and headings in plain words ("Approve treaty", "All treaties"). Use normal sentence case, not ALL CAPS.

Change the theme colour

Settings → Appearance lets you pick one accent colour that applies everywhere — far easier than styling each piece.

Loading & empty messages

Tables have built-in settings for "no rows yet" text and a loading spinner — turn them on so blank states don't look broken.

Add icons to buttons

Buttons have an icon setting — a checkmark on Approve, an upload arrow on the file button. A small, free touch.

Please avoid hand-written CSS or custom components during the hackathon — they eat time and break easily. Everything above is built into Retool's settings panels.

Plain-word dictionary

10 · Glossary

Quick, jargon-free meanings. Search to jump to one.

Treaty

The reinsurance contract your app reads. Signed between an insurer and a reinsurer (RGA).

ATF

The admin form/record where the treaty's details need to be entered. One place your data feeds into.

iRate

A pricing tool the rate tables get uploaded into. The other place your data feeds into.

product_info

The first output sheet — one row per product, with all the product fields. Your products table.

benefit_rate_set

The second output sheet — one row per benefit (net-risk calc, reprem, table, commission, rate_table_id). Shown in the drawer per product.

rate_table_id

A code that points a benefit to its rate table, like Addendum_11_RT1_M. It's built automatically from the amendment + the table number (the transformer does it).

Row action

A little button on each table row (e.g. "Details"). Clicking it can open the drawer for that row.

Rate table

The grid of pricing numbers by age/gender. For this hackathon we don't extract the grids themselves — benefits just carry a rate_table_id pointing to them.

App

The screen you're building in Retool — buttons, tables, and boxes.

Component

A drag-on piece: a table, a button, a file upload, a text box.

Query

An action the app runs: read a file, ask the AI, save to the database.

Resource

A connection the app uses — the AI service, the database. Set up by your admin.

Retool Database

A built-in place to store data in tables. Where treaties are saved.

GUI mode

A way to build a database action by filling in boxes instead of writing code. You'll use this to save and update.

AI action / Generate text

A query that asks an AI model to do something — here, pull the fields out of the treaty.

Convert document to text

The built-in AI query that reads the words out of a PDF. If it's not available, use the Workflow + Python "Plan B" in Step A2.

Workflow

A separate mini-program in Retool that runs a few steps in order, away from your app screen. We use one in Plan B to read the PDF's text with a small script.

pypdf (Python)

A free tool for reading PDFs. The Plan B Workflow uses it to pull the text out of a treaty. You just paste our script — no coding needed.

Transformer

A small helper that tidies data so it displays neatly. You copy ours in.

Event handler

A "when this happens, do that" rule. Example: when the AI finishes, save the treaty.

On success

The kind of event handler that runs after a query finishes without an error.

Drawer

A panel that slides in from the side to show details — here, a treaty's fields and rates.

Tabs (Tabbed Container)

A component with switchable tabs — an optional way to organise a busy drawer. (This build keeps the drawer simple with two stacked tables instead.)

SheetJS (XLSX)

A free tool you add once (a Library) that lets the app build an Excel file with multiple sheets. Used in the "Export to Excel" step.

Segmented Control

A row of buttons for picking one option — here, In Review / Approved / All.

JSON

A tidy way to write data as labelled values. The AI returns its answer as JSON; you rarely read it directly.

Validation

Automatic checks that the data looks right before it's approved.

Status (In Review / Approved)

A treaty's stage. New treaties are "In Review"; once a person confirms, they become "Approved" and lock.

Mock data

Practice/fake data. The only kind you're allowed to use in the hackathon.
